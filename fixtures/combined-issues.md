# Featured Research

## Research Brief

### Brief #1
**Title:** Economics of Encouragement: Can a Single Email Shape What Students Study?
**Authors:** Olivia Edwards & John Meer
**Summary:** Can a single email shape what students study? Among 10,000+ students in a large introductory economics course at Texas A&M, high-performing students who received a short note from a professor encouraging further study were more likely to enroll in the next economics course. The effect was largest for first-generation and underrepresented students — a low-cost nudge with real equity potential.
**Url:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1bhYRHaQLwsJ-9F5_5TN8BpW96Lz5rmSV/view?usp=sharing

### Brief #2
**Title:** Equity in Practice: How New Teachers Bring Fairness Into the Classroom
**Authors:** Andrew Kwok, Jenna Davis, Sara Halabi, Joseph Waddington, Debbee Huston & Rita Hemsley
**Summary:** Are new teachers prepared to teach fairly in increasingly diverse classrooms? Across nearly 1,900 survey responses from new teachers in one of California's largest induction programs, only a small share described their work with a clear equity focus. Those who did mapped a path from including diverse voices to addressing systemic inequities — concrete practices that preparation programs can teach.
**Url:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1M6BRphQD_OIEkL6qIWlphm4nC8dsjab4/view?usp=drive_link

### Brief #3
**Title:** Managing without Mandate: A Nationwide Review of State Training Laws
**Authors:** Megan Svajda-Hardy & Andrew Kwok
**Summary:** How much do state laws actually support teachers in managing classroom behavior? A review of discipline statutes across all 50 states and DC found only 31 statutes in 21 states that address support for general classroom teachers. Most name who provides training and who's covered, but few require it or specify how often — leaving classroom management largely unmandated.
**Url:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1mmxiUepPfojRo5BhW2y9hShfQWaHhRra/view?usp=drive_link

### Brief #4
**Title:** Into DEEP: Finding Schools Where Black Students Thrive in Texas
**Authors:** John A. Williams III, Sonyia C. Richardson & Chance W. Lewis
**Summary:** Black students in Texas face the highest suspension rates of any racial group and consistently underperform their peers on state assessments — which schools are bucking both trends? Using state and federal data from 2020-2021, researchers found only 87 of 3,988 urban Texas schools (2%) met benchmarks for both academic performance and proportional suspension rates for Black students. These "DEEP schools" — clustered near the Texas-Mexico border and around Houston and Dallas — had more diverse teaching staff than average, but most lacked full-time counselors, social workers, or psychologists. The findings show what's possible for Black students and point to where more investment is needed.
**Url:** https://erc.cehd.tamu.edu/publications-browser/

### Brief #5
**Title:** Distance to Degrees: How Community College Access Shapes Educational Opportunity in Texas
**Authors:** Riley K. Acton, Kalena E. Cortes, Lois Miller & Camila Morales
**Summary:** For many Texas students, the path to a college degree runs through a community college — but what happens when that college is an hour away? Using administrative data on more than 1.5 million high school graduates (2013-2017), this brief shows that students in "community college deserts" (more than 30 minutes from the nearest public institution) enroll and graduate at significantly lower rates. The effects fall hardest on Hispanic and lower-income students and persist for up to eight years, driven by distance, not by differences in academic preparation.
**Url:** https://drive.google.com/file/d/1YzVlGS5hH5RRvfacn851qWqNolkci0HF/preview

### Brief #6
**Title:** Can Intensive Advising Open Doors to Selective Colleges? Evidence from the EMERGE Fellowship
**Authors:** Brian Holzman, Irina Chukhray & Courtney Thrash
**Summary:** High-achieving, low-income students apply to selective colleges far less often than wealthier peers, even with similar academic records. Can the right kind of advising close that gap? This brief examines the EMERGE Fellowship, a multi-year college-access program in a large Texas school district, and finds that participants were 17 percentage points more likely to apply to selective colleges — with gains driven by advising rather than higher test scores.
**Url:** https://erc.cehd.tamu.edu/publications-browser/
